I. ORAN AIR BHREITH PHRIONNSA TEARLACH

AN naigheachd a fhuair sinn an dràsd’
A thàinig oirnn nuadh do’n tìr,
Chuir m’airtneal air chairtealan uam,
Dh’fhàg aigeantach, uallach mì;
Cha bhi sinn tuilleadh na’s mò
Aig Deòrsa fada fo chìs,
Thig sonas ri linn a’ Phrionns’ òig,
’S gheibh fir tha air fògradh sìth.

Rugadh Phoenix thall anns an Ròimh
Sgeul aigeantach mór ri ’linn;						10
Gum bi neart agus ceart mar ri treòir
Do’n fhear sheasas còir an Rìgh;
Théid a’ chuibhle fhathast mu’n cuairt,
’S am fear a tha shuas, bidh e sìos,
Bidh am fear a tha streapadh, gu h-ard,
’S fear eile gu làr tuitidh sìos.

III. ORAN AIR TEACHD PHRIONNSA TEARLACH

AN ulaidh phrìseil bha uainne
’S ann a fhuair sinn an dràsd’ ì,
Gum b’ì sud an leug bhuadhach
’Ga ceangal suas leis na gràsan;
Ged leig Dia greis air adhart
Do’n mhuic bhith cladhach ad àite,
Nis o’n thionndaidh a’ chuibhle
Théid gach traoitear fo’r sàiltean.

Slàn do’n t-saor rinn am bàta
A thug sàbhailt’ gu tìr thù;						10
Slàn do’n iùl-fhear neo-chearbach
Thug thar fairge gun dìth thù;
Gum b’è sud am preas toraidh
Thug an sonas do’n rìoghachd,
’S lìonmhor laoch thig fo d’ chaismeachd
Bheir air Sasunnaich strìochdadh.

Slàn do’n uachdaran ghasda
Dh’fhalbh o’n Cheapaich Dì-haoine,
Rinn an cruinneachadh rìoghail
Chuir fo fhiamh iad le maoidheadh;					20
Bha dà Dhomhnull ri d’ shlinnein,
’S do chuid gillean cha b’fhaoin iad
H-uile h-aon deas gu bualadh,
’S cha robh uath’ ach an saothair.

THE TEXT.

It is hardly too much to say that no satisfactory text of MacDonald’s poems has yet been produced. (2) Apart from the peculiarities of his own spelling—which represents nearly the first attempt to adapt the orthography of the old literary language common to Scotland and Ireland to the vernacular of the Highlands (3)—he uses forms which are now not employed in modern speech, and which have been consequently removed by all his editors from MacPherson onwards, presumably as a concession to readers unwilling to acquaint themselves with obsolete forms of the language. The most important of these are the genitive singular of the verbal noun in -te -the (still the normal Irish usage), the dative plural in -aibh or -ibh, (4) and the form ’nad for the second person of the prepositional-possessive pronoun. The last two occur uniformly in the first edition of Ais-Eiridh, and they have been nearly uniformly changed to -an and ’ad, forms which MacDonald certainly did not write, and cannot be presumed to have sung. The genitive of the verbal noun in -te, -the occurs here in the following passages:—

<gai>‘Tha sinne d’éis ar sàraichthe,
Ar nàraichth’, ’s móran sgìos oirnn’<eng> (X. 121, 122)

which means: ‘We have been oppressed, we have been humiliated, and we are very weary.’

NOTES ON THE POEMS TAKEN FROM MS. 63

AFTER the first proofs of this book had been printed, I was fortunately able to go to the Advocates’ Library in Edinburgh and to compare and revise with the original MS. all the poems in this anthology taken originally from the transcription that Professor MacKinnon first published in vols. iv. and v. of the Celtic Review.

This MS., of which Professor MacKinnon gives an account in vol. iv. of the Celtic Review and in his Catalogue of the Gaelic manuscripts in the Advocates’ Library, is presumed to have been written by Alexander MacDonald himself, between 1747 and 1751. Though all the poems in it except one are obviously by MacDonald, there is no direct proof that the manuscript is his, while the date of it is only deducible from internal evidence. It seems a rough draft of his first edition which appeared in 1751. Certainly some of the poems in the MS., which he did not include in his book, are much the worst of his work, while others which appear in both have their better version in the latter.

The MS. is defective, beginning at p. 118, while leaves are missing in other places. It is written in the old Gaelic script with the usual abbreviations. The handwriting is large, clear and legible, except near the edges of the paper which have become brown and frayed. The spelling employed resembles in certain respects that used in Ais-Eiridh, MacDonald’s first edition, but in others it is definitely different, being on the whole a good deal more crude. Thus, as in Ais-Eiridh, ar is used for ar and bhur, do for do and de, while the nasal is often omitted before s, l, m, n, r and f in the case of the article, preposition, and possessive pronoun an, etc. But the MS. has peculiarities which are not shared with Ais-Eiridh. The sound represented by the letter s in the word sinn, for instance, is in the MS. nearly always written sh, a confusing convention, while s and t aspirated are written h simply. Bho and its derivatives bhuam, etc., are written fo, fuam, etc. These usages appear to be whims of the writer’s. It is probable that systematic examination of the language and orthography of the MS. would disclose other similar affectations.
