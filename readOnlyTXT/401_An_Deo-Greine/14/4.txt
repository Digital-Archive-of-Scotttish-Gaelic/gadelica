“AN OLANN TU TABAC?”

Tha còrr is fichead bliadhna air dol seachad bho ’n cheud uair a chaidh Calum Mac Phàrlain do ’n Oireachtas am Bail-ath-cliath. Oidhche de na h-oidhcheannan thaghail Seosamh Laoide agus Tadhg O Donnchadha aige ’san tigh-òsda ’san robh e a’ fuireach aig an àm. (Tha ’m fear mu dheireadh de na seòid sin a nis ’na Ollamh ri Gàidhlig an Oil-thigh Baile Chorcaigh.) Air do ’n chéilidh a bh’ aig an triùir a bhi seachad, agus iad a’ dol a sìos an staidhir, agus Tadhg a’ cur lasair r’a phìob, ars esan ri Calum: “An ólann tu tabac?”—<eng>Do you drink tobacco?<gai> Fhreagair Calum: “Ni h-ólann”—<eng>I do not<gai>—agus thaisg e ’na mheamhair briathran an ràidh ro-annasaich ud: “An ólann tu tabac?”

An latha roimhe bha an Calum ceudna a’ leughadh mu Euchdan Réisimeid Mhic Aoidh an cogadh nan deich bliadhna fichead, mar thatar air an ìnnseadh le Seanalair Mac an Rothaich, agus thainig fo ’aire an t-iomradh so air sgiorra a thuit air cuid d’ a chàirdean uair a bha iad ri dos-long-phort mu ’n cuairt air Baile Oppenheim: <eng>“Sitting one night at supper a Bullet of thirty two pound weight, shot right out betwixt Colonell Hepburnes shoulder and mine, going through the Colonells coach; the next shot kill’d a sergeant of mine, by the fire, drinking a pipe of Tobacco.”<gai>

Bu mhaith leinne fios a bhi againn am bheil an gnàths-labhairt sin—<eng>drinking tobacco<gai>—ag ól tabac—dualach do ’n Ghàidhlig, no do ’n Bheurla Shasunnaich; no am bheil e beò, no an robh e riamh beò, air taobh a stigh de chrìochan na Gàidhlig an Albainn.

<eng>CELTIC TERMS OF INVECTIVE.<gai>

Sàr-dhubh do ghonaidh ort!<eng> The worst of bewitchment to you! This is an extremely intensified form of <gai>“Gonadh ort.” Sàr<eng> and <gai>dubh<eng> are both of strongly intensive force.

<gai>Seumachdan ort!<eng> Prohibitions upon you! <gai>Seum<eng>, or <gai>seàm<eng>, to forbid, enjoin, from which <gai>seumachd<eng> comes, seems to be simply a form of <gai>seun<eng>, to defend by enchantments, to refuse, deny. Hence the meaning of the saying, “May enchantments be upon you,” i.e., against you!

A minister in Duirnish, Skye, asked a woman in his flock if she ever made use of any bad words. She replied that she did not, except that once when her neighbour’s hens annoyed her she said to them, <gai>“Seumachdan oirbh,”<eng> or if they annoyed her very much, <gai>“Seachd seumachdan oirbh,”<eng> sevenfold enchantments on you!

<gai>Seun do ghonaidh ort!<eng> The enchantment of your blighting on you. Given by Nicolson as an alternative form of <gai>Sar-dhubh do ghonaidh ort.

Sian fala mu do shùilean!<eng> A shower of blood about your eyes. “Shower” is Nicolson’s rendering, but <gai>sian<eng> is a stronger word used of a tempest of wind and rain, or wind-driven rain. A “downpour” or “stream of blood” better conveys the force.

<gai>Siubhal a’ chait a chaidh do’n eas dhuit!<eng> The departure of the cat that went to the waterfall to you!

<gai>Siubhal Artair ort!<eng> Arthur’s departure to you!

<gai>Siubhal Mhurchaidh bho’n bhothan ort!<eng> Murdoch’s departure from the hut to you! <gai>Siubhal<eng> means going, moving, walking, and is also used of dying; <gai>shiubhail e<eng>, he died, he “departed.” The particular circumstances that gave point to these imprecations are not on record.

<gai>Siubhal na Samhna dha!<eng> The Hallowmass fate to you! Nicolson’s translation is “Let him go like Hallowmas.” The reference in the imprecation, however, is to the fate of the victim upon whom the demand for a human sacrifice at Hallowmas fell in the Celtic mythology. When the public rites about the <gai>Samhuinn<eng> or Hallowmass fire were concluded, everyone hastily made for home lest he should be the victim seized by the gods, but always the last of them was never seen again. <gai>Siubhal na Samhna<eng> had come upon him. Compare “<gai>An deireagan air an beir a bhéisd<eng>” and “<gai>Am fear a bhios air dheireadh beiridh a’ bhéist air.”

Suaineas ort!<eng> Wrapping on you (i.e., dead-wrapping)! Reay. <gai>Suaineas fion suain<eng>, to wreathe round.

<gai>Tìgh do sheanar dhuit!<eng> Your grandfather’s house to you!

<gai>Tigh Eóghainn mhic lain bhuidhe dhuit!<eng> The house of Ewen son of yellow John to you! The two last sayings evidently allude to the grave.
