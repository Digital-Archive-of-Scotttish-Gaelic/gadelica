The Copula

Forms of the copula

The form of the copula depends on three different factors:

  • whether the clause has positive or negative polarity
  • whether the clause is declarative or interrogative
  • whether the clause has present/future tense or past/conditional tense.

The forms of the copula are presented in the following table:

  present/future past/conditional
  declarative interrogative declarative interrogative
positive is an bu am bu
negative cha nach cha bu nach bu

Notes:

  • is is often written as ’s.
  • an becomes am before labial consonants, i.e. b-, f-, m-, p-.
  • cha becomes chan before a vowel.
  • bu is usually written as b’ before a vowel.

Equative clauses – copula + e + definite noun + definite noun

An equative clause contains two definite nouns and states that they refer to (or denote) the same entity. In modern Scottish Gaelic, the standard way of expressing an equative clause involves using the following pattern:

  • a form of the copula, followed by:
  • a ‘dummy’ third person singular pronoun e, followed by:
  • a definite noun (or noun phrase), followed by:
  • another definite noun (or noun phrase).

Definite nouns can be either:

  • emphatic personal pronouns, i.e. mise, thusa, esan, ise, sinne, sibhse, iadsan
  • demonstrative pronouns, i.e. seo, sin, siud
  • proper nouns, e.g. Iain, Màiri
  • specified commons nouns, i.e. taking a definite or personal article or a following definite genitive, e.g. an tidsear, mo mhàthair, bean a’ mhinistear.
